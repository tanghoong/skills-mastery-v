# Chapter 18 — Talking with Juniors

## Why It Matters

"Junior" here means significantly younger than you — younger colleagues, interns,
teenagers, early-career people, younger family members.

It's surprisingly easy to get this wrong. People default to one of two bad modes:
being condescending without realizing it, or trying too hard to seem relatable.
Both make the other person uncomfortable.

The goal is to be genuinely curious about where they are and what they see —
because they're often seeing things you can't from where you stand.

---

## Core Dynamics to Understand

### 1. The Condescension Trap

Condescension rarely feels like condescension from the inside. It shows up as:
- Explaining things they already know (without checking first)
- Adding "at your age" or "when you're older" to sentences
- Giving advice before it's asked for
- Treating their views as incomplete versions of yours
- Nodding along to their ideas with a "that's cute" energy

**How to avoid it:** Ask before you explain. "Do you want my take on this, or are you just thinking out loud?"
And treat their ideas as fully formed — which means engaging with them directly, disagreeing when
you genuinely disagree, and being willing to be convinced.

### 2. They're Not Confused — They're Different

Younger generations aren't simply earlier, less experienced versions of you.
They've grown up in a genuinely different world:
- Digital-native (some have never known a world without smartphones)
- Different relationship to employment, institutions, and authority
- Exposed to more information at a younger age
- Different cultural references, humor, and values

This doesn't mean every difference is correct — but it means their perspective is a real data point,
not a mistake to be corrected.

**Ask genuinely:** "How do you think about [topic]?" rather than waiting to correct their answer.

### 3. The Power Dynamic

In professional settings especially, the junior person may be filtering what they say to you
based on your seniority. They may not disagree, may not share problems, may perform agreement.

Creating psychological safety requires signals:
- Admitting uncertainty or mistakes yourself
- Responding to pushback with interest rather than defensiveness
- Explicitly inviting their real opinion ("I genuinely want to know what you think, not what you think I want to hear")
- Not reacting badly when they tell you something you don't want to hear

The people who create genuine safety with juniors are the ones who get the most honest, useful feedback.

### 4. They Know Things You Don't

This is the most important shift. Younger people are often first-contact with new cultural trends,
technologies, platforms, and ideas. They understand how Gen Z or Gen Alpha actually uses things —
not how marketers describe them.

Reverse-mentoring is real: ask them what they're using, what they think is overrated,
what's actually changing in [your field]. You might be surprised.

## What to Talk About

### Topics That Work Well
- What they're working on and genuinely excited about
- What they're learning and what's surprising them
- Their perspective on things in your shared domain (work, family, culture)
- Their generation's take on things you think you understand
- Their goals — not just the immediate ones, but the deeper ones

### Questions That Build Real Conversation
- "What's the most interesting thing you've worked on recently?"
- "What do you think most people your age get wrong about [topic]?"
- "What do you think is overrated? And underrated?"
- "What are you trying to get better at right now?"
- "What would you do differently if you were running [thing] right now?"

### Avoid
- "Back in my day..." (immediate distance creator)
- "When you get older you'll understand..." (dismissive)
- Explaining what TikTok/Instagram/their platform is if they clearly use it
- Complaining about their generation to them
- Giving unsolicited career or life advice

---

## The "What's It Like" Question

Just as "what was it like when...?" works with seniors — "what's it like to be...?" works with juniors.

"What's it like to have grown up with social media from day one?"
"What's it like to be entering [field] right now — how does it feel from where you're standing?"
"What's it like to be 22 and looking at the next few years?"

These questions signal genuine curiosity about their perspective rather than the assumption
that you already know what it's like.

---

## In Professional / Mentorship Settings

If you're a senior person mentoring or managing younger colleagues:
- Listen before advising — understand the situation fully before offering solutions
- Share your own mistakes as much as your successes — it normalizes failure and builds trust
- Give them real responsibility and real credit — don't manage from above
- Ask for their feedback on you explicitly — and actually use it

---

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | What to do instead |
|---------|-------------------|
| Explaining without checking | Ask "do you know much about this?" before explaining |
| "At your age..." | Talk about your experience without implying theirs is lesser |
| Giving advice constantly | Ask what they want first — help, or just someone to listen? |
| Treating pop culture gaps as deficits | Ask them to fill you in — they're usually happy to |
| Assuming they're impressionable | Treat their views as fully formed; engage and push back |

---

## Practice Prompts

1. Have a conversation with someone significantly younger where your only goal is to learn
   something about the world from their perspective. No advice. Just questions and listening.

2. Next time a junior person disagrees with you, respond with "tell me more" before
   explaining or defending your position.

3. Ask a younger person: "What do people my age completely misunderstand about people your age?"
   Then actually listen without defending.

---

## Quick Reference

| Dynamic | How to navigate it |
|---------|-------------------|
| Condescension | Ask before explaining; engage with their ideas directly |
| Power dynamic | Signal safety: admit mistakes, invite honest pushback |
| Generational difference | Curiosity, not correction — "what's it like for you?" |
| They know things you don't | Ask about what they're seeing, using, and thinking |
| Advice | Ask if they want it first. Then give it briefly. |
