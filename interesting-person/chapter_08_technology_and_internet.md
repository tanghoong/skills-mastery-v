# Chapter 8 — Technology & the Internet

## Why It Matters

Technology is the shared language of modern life. Even non-technical people use it constantly.
Tech facts land well because they're often counterintuitive ("wait, that was invented in...?"),
and they explain things people interact with every day but never think about.

You don't need to be a developer to use these facts. You just need to know the stories.

---

## How to Use Tech Facts in Conversation

Tech works best as **origin stories** and **scale comparisons**:
- "You know that app started as something completely different, right?"
- "The scale of this thing is hard to even visualize..."
- "Most people don't realize how old/young [technology] actually is."

## The Facts (Ready to Use)

### Origin Stories (Not What You Think)

**Instagram was originally a whiskey app.**
The first version was called Burbn — a location check-in app for whiskey enthusiasts.
The founders kept only one feature that users actually used: photo sharing.
That became Instagram. Pivoting saved them.

**Twitter's 140-character limit came from SMS.**
SMS messages were capped at 160 characters. Twitter allocated 140 for the message
and 20 for the username. When the SMS constraint disappeared, they doubled to 280.
A product constraint from the 2000s shaped how the world communicates for over a decade.

**YouTube was originally a dating site.**
The domain youtube.com was registered in 2005 with a video dating concept — "tune in, hook up."
Nobody uploaded dating videos. People uploaded everything else.
The founders pivoted. Less than 18 months later, Google bought it for $1.65 billion.

**The @ symbol was chosen for email by accident.**
Ray Tomlinson chose @ in 1971 because it appeared on keyboards, was rarely used in names,
and meant "at" as in "user at server." He later said he chose it almost arbitrarily.
That one choice became the defining symbol of digital communication.

**QWERTY was not designed for speed — it was designed to reduce typewriter jams.**
The layout separated commonly used letter pairs to prevent mechanical keys from sticking.
We optimized our fingers around a mechanical problem that hasn't existed for 80 years.

### Scale That's Hard to Comprehend

**Every 2 days, we create as much information as was created in all of human history up to 2003.**
That was true in 2010 according to Google CEO Eric Schmidt. The rate has only accelerated.

**Google processes about 8.5 billion searches per day.**
That's roughly 100,000 per second. The sum of human curiosity, measured in real time.

**The Internet weighs about 50 grams.**
Physicist Russell Seitz calculated the mass of all the electrons in motion constituting
internet traffic at any given moment — about 50 grams, the weight of a strawberry.
The information that holds civilization together weighs less than your coffee cup.

**If you stacked all the transistors ever made on top of each other, the pile would reach the moon.**
Modern chips contain billions of transistors. The density of computing power
is almost impossible to visualize at human scale.

### Weird History of Computing

**The first computer "bug" was an actual bug.**
In 1947, Grace Hopper's team found a moth stuck in a relay of the Harvard Mark II computer.
They taped it into their log with the note "First actual case of bug being found."
The word "bug" for software error already existed, but this made it concrete.

**The Apollo 11 computer had less processing power than a modern calculator.**
The Apollo Guidance Computer ran at 0.043 MHz with 4KB of RAM.
A basic Texas Instruments calculator is about 350,000 times more powerful.
The Moon landing ran on less computing power than your digital watch.

**The first webcam was pointed at a coffee pot.**
Researchers at Cambridge's Computer Science department in 1991 were tired of walking
to the break room to find an empty pot. They set up a camera to check remotely.
The coffee pot cam became one of the first widely watched live streams on the internet.

**Before the Mac, the only way to interact with a computer was text commands.**
The graphical interface (windows, icons, mouse pointer) was not obvious.
Early Apple employees didn't believe consumers would understand or want it.
Steve Jobs saw a demo at Xerox PARC and immediately knew it was the future.

### Modern Tech Oddities

**The guy who invented pop-up ads apologized.**
Ethan Zuckerman, who created one of the early implementations, wrote a public apology
in 2014, calling it "one of the most hated tools in the advertiser's toolkit."
He built it to give brands exposure without associating them with potentially controversial content.
It backfired spectacularly.

**Amazon's recommendation algorithm was originally called "similarities."**
The "people who bought X also bought Y" system — now worth billions in sales influence —
started as a simple collaborative filter. It's now one of Amazon's biggest revenue drivers.

**The Wi-Fi logo has no technical meaning.**
The symbol (three arcing lines) was designed for aesthetics. It doesn't represent radio waves,
signal strength, or anything meaningful. It just looked like it represented wireless connectivity.

**No country code top-level domain has been used more creatively than .io**
Originally assigned to the British Indian Ocean Territory, it became the preferred domain
for tech startups ("input/output" in programmer language). The actual territory has a
complicated geopolitical history that the startup world cheerfully ignores.
