# Chapter 7 — Psychology & Human Behavior

## Why It Matters

Psychology is the ultimate conversation domain because it's about *people* — and everyone
is interested in why they and the people around them do the things they do.

A good psychology fact doesn't just entertain: it gives someone a new lens for their own life.
That's genuinely valuable. People remember you when you give them something useful to think about.

---

## How to Use Psychology Facts in Conversation

Psychology works best when it's personal:
- "Have you ever noticed you do this? There's actually a name for it..."
- "There's a reason that happens — it's actually well-studied."
- "This explains why [thing that happened to both of you] feels the way it does."

Make it about *them*, not a lecture. Invite them to recognize themselves in the idea.

## The Facts (Ready to Use)

### Cognitive Biases (The Shortcuts That Fool Us)

**The Dunning-Kruger Effect**
People with limited knowledge in a domain tend to overestimate their competence.
People with high expertise tend to underestimate it.
The least-informed voices are often the most confident. This is why expertise makes you humble
and ignorance makes you certain.

**Confirmation Bias**
We unconsciously seek out information that confirms what we already believe, and
dismiss information that contradicts it. You're not immune to this. Neither am I.
The terrifying part: it happens even when we're trying to be objective.

**The Spotlight Effect**
You overestimate how much other people notice your mistakes, appearance, or behavior.
Everyone is too busy thinking about themselves to notice you as much as you think.
This one is genuinely useful for social anxiety — your stumble was not as visible as it felt.

**The Bystander Effect**
The more people present in an emergency, the less likely any individual is to help.
Everyone assumes someone else will act. The way to counter it: make direct eye contact
with one specific person and say "You — can you call 911?"

**Anchoring**
The first number you see shapes your perception of all numbers that follow.
A product "marked down from $500 to $200" feels like a deal even if $200 is overpriced.
Salary negotiations use this — whoever says the first number sets the anchor.

### Social & Relationship Psychology

**The Mere Exposure Effect**
Simply being exposed to something repeatedly makes you like it more —
even if you didn't particularly like it at first.
This is why songs grow on you. And why you tend to become friends with people
you see often, regardless of initial chemistry.

**The Ben Franklin Effect**
Asking someone for a small favor makes them like you *more*, not less.
Helping you causes them to rationalize: "I must like this person, or why would I help them?"
If you want someone to warm up to you: ask them for a small, easy favor.

**Reciprocity**
If someone does something for you, you feel compelled to return it — even if you didn't ask
for the original favor. Marketers use this constantly (free samples, free trials).
In friendship: genuine generosity creates genuine connection.

**The 36 Questions**
Psychologist Arthur Aron designed a set of 36 questions intended to make two strangers
fall in love. The experiment worked — repeatedly. The secret: mutual vulnerability escalates
gradually. The questions start easy ("What would be your perfect day?") and get progressively
deeper ("When did you last cry, and why?"). Pairs who went through all 36 questions often
reported feeling closer than people who'd known each other for years.

**Love Languages (Briefly)**
Gary Chapman's framework — Acts of Service, Words of Affirmation, Quality Time,
Gift Giving, Physical Touch — is pop psychology but surprisingly useful for
diagnosing relationship friction. Two people showing love in different languages
often feel unloved despite loving each other genuinely.

### Memory & Perception

**The Mandela Effect**
Large groups of people collectively misremember the same things.
Famous examples: many people remember the Monopoly Man having a monocle (he doesn't),
Darth Vader saying "Luke, I am your father" (the actual line is "No, I am your father"),
and Looney Tunes being spelled "Looney Toons" (it's not).
Memory is reconstruction, not recording.

**The Peak-End Rule**
You don't remember experiences as averages. You remember them by their peak moment
and their ending. A painful medical procedure remembered as "not that bad"
if the pain reduced gradually at the end — even if the total pain was higher.
This has implications for how you end conversations, dates, and meetings.

**Change Blindness**
People often fail to notice significant changes in their visual field if the change
occurs during a brief distraction (a blink, a cut in video, someone handing something to you).
In famous experiments, a researcher asking for directions was secretly swapped with
a different person mid-conversation — and most people didn't notice.
We see far less than we think we do.

### Why People Do What They Do

**Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation**
Rewarding someone for doing something they already enjoy often kills the enjoyment.
In studies, children who loved drawing were given rewards for it — and stopped drawing
voluntarily when the rewards stopped. External rewards undermine internal motivation.
This is called the "overjustification effect."

**The IKEA Effect**
People value things more when they've built or created them, even if the result
is objectively worse than a ready-made alternative.
This explains why people are irrationally attached to their own business ideas,
their own cooking, and their own solutions.

**Terror Management Theory**
A lot of human behavior — ambition, religion, art, legacy-seeking, tribalism —
is driven by the unconscious need to manage the terror of knowing we will die.
We create meaning systems (nations, religions, art) partly as a buffer against mortality.
Heavy, but it explains a surprising amount.

## Conversation Starters

- "Have you heard of the Spotlight Effect? It basically proves no one is paying as much attention to you as you think."
- "There's this thing called the Ben Franklin Effect — asking someone a small favor makes them like you more. Which sounds backwards but actually makes psychological sense."
- "The Peak-End Rule explains why how you end a date matters more than everything else combined."
