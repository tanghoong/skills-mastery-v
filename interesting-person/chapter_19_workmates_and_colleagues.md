# Chapter 19 — Workmates & Colleagues

## Why It Matters

You spend more waking hours with colleagues than almost anyone else in your life.
And yet professional relationships are often the most surface-level, most awkward,
and most missed opportunities for genuine connection.

Small talk with workmates doesn't have to be painful. Professional networks don't have to feel
fake. And conversations with your boss or team can be genuinely interesting.
This chapter covers how to navigate the unique dynamics of workplace conversation.

---

## Core Dynamics to Understand

### 1. The Professional Filter

Work relationships carry implicit rules: don't say too much, don't share too much,
don't make things weird. This filter is useful — but it's often set too high,
making people seem bland, evasive, or uninterested when they're actually just cautious.

The goal is calibrated openness: enough personality to be real, not so much that it
creates discomfort or professional risk.

**Rule of thumb:** You can share more than you think. Most people are relieved when
someone else goes first with a bit of genuine personality.

### 2. Hierarchy Affects Communication

**Talking with someone senior to you:**
- Be direct and confident — mealy-mouthed language signals low status
- Don't only talk when addressed — initiate occasionally
- Ask about their perspective on things outside your immediate work overlap
- Disagree carefully but actually disagree when you genuinely do
- Show curiosity about their career and how they think, not just their decisions

**Talking with someone junior to you:**
See Chapter 18 — everything there applies here, especially the condescension trap.

**Talking with peers:**
Most natural context. Banter, genuine problems, shared frustration, collaborative ideas.
Peers are where the most honest relationships are built.

### 3. Shared Context Is an Asset

You have a built-in conversation starter that strangers don't: the same workplace,
the same projects, the same frustrations, the same company culture.

This shared context means:
- Inside observations land immediately ("that meeting was something else")
- Shared frustrations create bonding (complaining about the same things, carefully)
- Shared victories are genuine shared experiences

Use it — but don't make all your conversation about work. That's how you become someone
whose only dimension in the office is their job title.

### 4. The Lunch-Table Test

The easiest place to convert a colleague relationship from functional to genuine is
during unstructured time — lunch, coffee runs, waiting for meetings to start.

These moments are underutilized. Most people check their phone. The person who puts
their phone away, notices the person next to them, and asks a real question stands out
within a week.

## What to Talk About

### Starter Topics (Safe, Builds Context)
- What they're working on currently (not their job description — what's actually occupying them)
- Something recent in the company, industry, or news
- Plans for the weekend or upcoming time off
- Something you've been reading, listening to, or thinking about

### Topics That Build Real Relationships
- What they actually want to be doing (vs. what they're currently doing)
- What they find most frustrating about the work or environment
- Something that surprised them recently
- Their career path — the decisions, pivots, and moments of uncertainty
- Their life outside work — what they do, what they're into, who they are

### Navigating Office Gossip
Gossip is a bonding mechanism — it's almost impossible to avoid entirely.
But there are types:

**Fine:** Speculating about company direction, laughing at shared absurdities, discussing
interpersonal dynamics in general terms.

**Risky:** Specific criticism of specific colleagues that could be repeated.

**Rule:** If you wouldn't say it in front of the person, be careful. Assume it will get back.

### Avoiding Conversation Killers at Work
- "I'm so busy" — relatable but closes down the conversation
- Constant negativity about the company — exhausting and marks you
- Turning every topic back to your own work — narcissistic pattern people notice
- TMI too fast — oversharing personal drama with people you're still building trust with

## Navigating Networking (Work Events, Conferences)

Networking events are awkward for almost everyone — the format is artificial and everyone
knows it. Using that as shared ground actually works:

- "I always feel like I have no idea how to do these things — how do you approach them?"
- "What brings you to this one?"
- "What's been the most useful conversation you've had today?"

Focus on one or two genuinely interesting conversations rather than collecting business cards.
A real 15-minute conversation is worth more than 20 surface-level exchanges.

**Follow-up is where networking actually happens.** A brief, specific message within 48 hours
("It was great talking about X — I'd love to stay in touch / continue that conversation")
is more valuable than the event itself.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | What to do instead |
|---------|-------------------|
| Only talking work | Ask about the person beyond their job — they're more than their role |
| Constant complaining | Share frustrations selectively; balance with curiosity and positivity |
| Gossip that burns | Keep specific criticism close to the vest |
| Phone during unstructured time | Put it away — it signals you'd rather not talk |
| Networking card-collecting | One real conversation beats ten surface exchanges |

---

## Ice-Breakers for Work Contexts

- "What are you working on right now that's actually interesting?"
- "Have you been here [at this company / in this industry] long? What's changed?"
- "I've been meaning to ask — what do you actually do on [that project / team]?"
- "Did you catch what they said in the [meeting / announcement] about [X]? What did you make of it?"
- "What do you do when you're not here — do you have a life outside this place?" (said lightly)

---

## Practice Prompts

1. This week, have one non-work conversation with a colleague you normally only discuss work with.
   Ask them about something in their life outside the office.

2. In the next unstructured work moment (lunch, coffee line, pre-meeting waiting), put your
   phone away and talk to whoever's near you. Notice what opens up.

3. Think of a senior colleague you'd like a better relationship with. What's one question
   from this chapter you could genuinely ask them this week?

## Quick Reference

| Context | Approach |
|---------|---------|
| With peers | Shared context, genuine banter, real problems — most natural |
| With seniors | Direct, confident, curious about how they think — not just what they decide |
| With juniors | Ask before advising, psychological safety, genuine curiosity |
| Unstructured time | Phone away, one real question, listen to the full answer |
| Networking events | One real conversation > ten cards; follow up within 48 hours |
