# Chapter 21 — Asking Great Questions

## Why It Matters

The most underrated skill in conversation is not talking — it's asking.

Great questions do something remarkable: they make the other person feel interesting.
They give permission to go deeper than surface talk. They make *you* more interesting
by proxy — because someone who asks thoughtful questions is clearly a thoughtful person.

And practically: you can have a genuinely great conversation knowing almost nothing
about the topic being discussed, if you know how to ask the right questions.

---

## The Anatomy of a Great Question

**A great question:**
1. Is open-ended (can't be answered with yes/no)
2. Invites a real answer, not a correct one
3. Signals genuine curiosity
4. Goes slightly deeper than the surface level

**A bad question:**
- "Did you enjoy it?" (yes/no, closed)
- "So what do you do?" (first question, reduces them to their job)
- "Don't you think..." (leading — seeks agreement, not their actual view)
- "Have you ever thought about X?" (often just wanting to talk about X yourself)

---

## Three Types of Questions

### Type 1 — Factual Questions
Ask about what happened, what they did, what the situation is.
Entry-level, but necessary to establish context.

"How long have you been working there?"
"Where did you go?"
"What did they say?"

### Type 2 — Reflective Questions
Ask about what they think, feel, or believe about the situation.
This is where conversation starts becoming real.

"What did you make of that?"
"How did you feel about it at the time?"
"What surprised you most?"

### Type 3 — Generative Questions
Ask questions that create new thinking — for them, not just reveal existing thoughts.
These are the rarest and most powerful.

"If you could go back and do it differently, what would you change?"
"What would you have to believe for that to make sense?"
"What does this tell you about what you actually want?"

Generative questions are not interrogating — they're thinking together.
Use them when trust is established and the conversation has depth.

---

## The Follow-Up Is Everything

Most people ask a question, get an answer, and move to the next question.
This creates the feeling of an interview, not a conversation.

The better move: respond to the answer before asking the next question.

**Interview mode:**
- You: "What do you enjoy most about your job?"
- Them: "Honestly, the problem-solving — I love when something's broken and I get to fix it."
- You: "Oh cool. How long have you been there?"

**Conversation mode:**
- You: "What do you enjoy most about your job?"
- Them: "Honestly, the problem-solving — I love when something's broken and I get to fix it."
- You: "That's specific — is it the complexity you're drawn to, or the moment it clicks?"
- Them: *now actually engaged*

The follow-up question uses something they just said. It signals you're listening, not waiting.

## Questions That Almost Always Work

### For someone you just met:
- "What's been the most interesting thing you've worked on lately?"
- "What do you do that most people don't know about?"
- "What made you choose [their city / career / this event]?"

### For someone you're getting to know:
- "What's something you changed your mind about recently?"
- "What's something most people get wrong about [topic they care about]?"
- "If you could be doing anything in 5 years, what would it be?"

### For deeper connection:
- "What's something you're proud of that most people wouldn't know?"
- "When did you feel most like yourself?"
- "What's something you wish you could tell your younger self?"

### For intellectual conversation:
- "What's the most interesting idea you've encountered lately?"
- "What do you think most people misunderstand about [topic]?"
- "Is there anything you used to believe confidently that you're not sure about anymore?"

### The 36 Questions (Arthur Aron) — Selected
These are designed to build deep connection fast through mutual vulnerability:
- "Given the choice of anyone in the world, who would you want as a dinner guest?"
- "What would constitute a perfect day for you?"
- "When did you last sing to yourself? To someone else?"
- "If you knew that in one year you would die suddenly, what would you change about the way you're living?"
- "What, if anything, is too serious to be joked about?"

---

## When to Ask, When to Stop

Asking too many questions in a row starts to feel interrogative — even if each question
is good. Balance asking with sharing.

**Rule:** For every 2–3 questions, offer something personal in return.
Not a topic change — a related share.

"You mentioned you felt stuck at the time — I had something similar happen in [situation].
It's a weird feeling when..."

This reciprocity signals that the conversation is mutual, not extractive.

---

## Questions to Avoid

| Question | Why to avoid | Better alternative |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| "What do you do?" (first question) | Reduces them to their job | "What have you been working on lately?" |
| "Don't you think...?" | Seeks agreement, not their view | "What do you think about...?" |
| "Why did you do that?" (accusatory tone) | Puts them on defense | "What was going through your mind?" |
| Multiple questions at once | Confusing, dilutes each one | Ask one, listen, follow up |
| Questions with obvious answers | Patronizing | Only ask if you're genuinely curious |

## Practice Prompts

1. In your next significant conversation, consciously move from Type 1 to Type 2 questions.
   Notice how the dynamic changes when you ask "what did you make of it?" instead of just
   "what happened?"

2. Write 5 questions you'd genuinely like to ask someone important to you. Choose one to
   actually ask this week.

3. Count how many questions you asked in your last real conversation vs. how many you asked
   follow-up questions on. The ratio tells you a lot.

## Quick Reference

| Type | What it does | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Factual | Establishes context | "What happened next?" |
| Reflective | Reveals thinking and feeling | "What did you make of that?" |
| Generative | Creates new thinking together | "What would you do differently?" |
